The Chimu were successful at conquest because they incorporated local nobility into their ranks and built their religious traditions to incorporate those of the earlier local civilization, the Moche's, including the Staff God. Another contributor to their successful expansion was that the Chimu had an extensive irrigation system, controlling the Moche, Chicama, and Viru valleys. The Chimu canal system irrigated 201.4 square kilometers of land, more than present day (Moore & Mackey 2008).

The Chimu religion had four main deities, the Staff God, depicted holding two staffs, the Plumed Headdress God which was depicted holding a severed head and a tumi knife, the Moon Animal which combines feline and fox features and rides the moon, and the Chimu Goddess Deity who is always depicted with references to the ocean, pelicans, fish, and sometimes sharks and rays. She is sometimes depicted holding yarn, spindles, or infants in cradleboards. The sea god Ni was important to Chimu tradition as well, and offerings were made of white maize flower and red ochre to ask for good fishing and to be safe from drowning.
    The Chimu left a legacy of elaborate adobe buildings and palaces, ceramic vessels, and metalwork with murals and reliefs depicting ocean creatures, especially pelicans, Spondylus shells, and their deities (Scher 2017). According to the remaining archeological and architectural evidence, in the Chimu capital city of Chan Chan, nine adobe palaces were built where Chimu kings did their administrative work and stored food and supplies. These same palaces became their mausoleums after death (Moseley 1975).*
